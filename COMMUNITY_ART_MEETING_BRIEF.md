# Community Art Project Meeting Brief

Date: Wednesday, 29 April 2026

## One-Line Pitch

HistoryLens is a collaborative local-history map for turning shopfronts, streets, old names, photos, archive links, memories, and research leads into a moderated public timeline.

## What To Show

1. Open the Waterloo Road project.
2. Pan around the map and show that each place has a location, category, and timeline state.
3. Open one place, then show:
   - Summary
   - Timeline entries
   - Historic names
   - Change history
   - Talk/comments
4. Add or suggest a timeline entry with a structured source.
5. Show Project Settings/moderation if signed in as an owner/admin.
6. Explain that research leads are intentionally marked as leads until verified.

## Why It Fits A Community Art Project

- It lets residents contribute fragments without needing to write a polished article.
- It keeps local knowledge tied to real places on a map.
- It supports creative outputs: walks, plaques, exhibitions, zines, audio trails, projected maps, and school workshops.
- It separates speculation from verified sources, which is important for public history.
- It gives moderators a review path before community submissions become published facts.

## Strong Talking Points

- "We can capture what people remember, then gradually attach evidence."
- "A place can have many lives: old names, different businesses, photos, comments, and corrections."
- "The map is not just a display. It is a research workspace."
- "The workflow supports open community contribution without giving up editorial control."
- "This could become a living archive for the project, not just a one-off exhibition asset."

## Good Questions To Ask Them

- Which streets, buildings, shops, or venues matter most to the community art project?
- Do they want public submissions, invited contributors, or a small editorial group first?
- What kinds of material will people bring: photos, memories, clippings, maps, oral history?
- Who should review suggestions before publication?
- What final output do they imagine: exhibition map, printed trail, website, workshops, QR codes, or archive?

## Practical Next Steps

1. Pick one pilot area and 10 to 20 seed places.
2. Agree on contribution rules: what counts as verified, likely, and speculative.
3. Add 2 or 3 example entries per place before inviting wider participation.
4. Recruit a small reviewer group.
5. Decide whether the project should stay private during collection or be public from day one.
